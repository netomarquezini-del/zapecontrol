## 1. DAVID OGILVY — The Ogilvy Formula {#1-david-ogilvy}

### Core Mental Model: "The Ogilvy Formula Layout"
Every ad must contain exactly 5 elements in this order:
1. **Visual** (image) — dominates ~75% of the ad, ALWAYS at the top
2. **Caption** — immediately below the image
3. **Headline** — bold, large typeface, below the caption
4. **Copy** — ~240 words, editorial style
5. **Signature** — brand/contact details

### Specific Design Rules

**On Images:**
- Image dominates 3/4 of the ad space
- Image ALWAYS at the top of the layout
- Photos of finished results outperform photos of raw ingredients
- Images that arouse CURIOSITY work hardest
- Show the product in use, not just the product alone
- Photographs beat illustrations in almost every test

**On Headlines:**
- "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy"
- Headlines BELOW the image get 10% more readers than headlines above
- NEVER capitalize entire headlines — eyes read all-caps one letter at a time
- Include brand name in the headline (80% who skip body copy still see it)
- Never use tricky headlines, puns, or literary allusions — "this is a sin"
- Telegraph what you want to say — clarity over cleverness

**On Typography:**
- Serif fonts for body copy (easier to read)
- Sans-serif for headlines
- Maximum 2 typefaces per ad
- Drop caps increase readership by 13%
- Use upper and lowercase, never all-caps for body

**On Layout:**
- Lead off with your brand name
- Don't be afraid to show product packaging
- Specifics work better than generalities (numbers > vague claims)
- Captions under photos are read 2x more than body copy

### What Makes Ogilvy Unique
Research-obsessed. Every rule comes from tested data, not opinion. He treated advertising as science, not art. His approach prioritizes the IMAGE as the primary attention-getter and the HEADLINE as the primary selling tool.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
OGILVY_RULES = {
  layout_order: ["image_top", "caption", "headline", "copy", "signature"],
  image_ratio: 0.75,  // 75% of ad space
  headline_position: "below_image",
  headline_case: "sentence_case",  // NEVER all-caps
  max_typefaces: 2,
  headline_font: "sans-serif",
  body_font: "serif",
  must_include: ["brand_name_in_headline", "product_visible", "specific_numbers"],
  never_do: ["puns_in_headlines", "all_caps", "headline_above_image", "abstract_art"]
}
```

## 2. LUKE SULLIVAN — Visual Concepting {#2-luke-sullivan}

### Core Mental Model: "The Visual IS the Ad"
A great ad could contain few or no words — just a picture and the brand name. The visual should carry the entire concept.

### Key Frameworks

**The Tension/Polarity Framework:**
- The fastest way to a big idea is leveraging creative tensions
- Use polarities to spark story (before/after, problem/solution, expectation/reality)
- Conflict creates interest — show the tension, not just the resolution

**The Simplicity Doctrine:**
- "When you have it just right, the strategy should be evident in the campaign, but the campaign should not be evident in the strategy"
- Simplicity in a cluttered media landscape = competitive advantage
- Fewer words, stronger visual = more memorable

**Concepting Techniques:**
- Start with straightforward expression — get something on paper without fear of imperfection
- Use images, lists, and symbols to spur creativity
- Nurture ideas before critiquing them
- Visuals serve the CORE INTENTION — never decoration

### Classic Examples of Sullivan's Approach
- **Jeep ad**: No car shown. Just a boat and trailer coated in mud with Jeep logo in corner. The ABSENCE tells the story.
- **Volkswagen park assist**: Empty parking spot between a cop car and motorcycles. Product benefit communicated purely through visual.

### What Makes Sullivan Unique
The visual carries the concept alone. Copy is secondary or absent. The brand story is told through a single, clever image that makes the viewer think. He values IDEA over execution.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
SULLIVAN_RULES = {
  visual_carries_concept: true,
  minimize_text: true,  // ideally 0-5 words
  use_tension: true,  // show contrast, conflict, surprise
  brand_placement: "subtle_corner",
  storytelling: "implication_over_explanation",
  test_question: "Does this image work WITHOUT any text?",
  avoid: ["decoration", "clip_art_thinking", "literal_product_shots_only"]
}
```

## 3. JOANNA WIEBE — Conversion Copywriting Visual Principles {#3-joanna-wiebe}

### Core Mental Model: "The Rule of One"
Every piece of conversion creative must have:
1. **One Reader** — write to ONE specific person
2. **One Big Idea** — a single dominant concept
3. **One Offer** — one thing you're selling
4. **One Promise** — one outcome they'll get

### The Complementary Framework: "List, Offer, Copy"
- **List** = your reader (who)
- **Offer** = your one offer (what)
- **Copy** = your big idea + promise (why)

### Visual Design Principles from Wiebe

**On Images:**
- A photo of a woman outperforms a photo of a man in hero sections
- Images of people looking at camera create connection
- Put the most important visual where attention naturally falls

**On Headlines in Visuals:**
- Put headlines in FIRST PERSON and in QUOTATION MARKS
- First-person voice ("I finally..." / "My skin...") creates identification
- Quotation marks signal a real person's testimony

**On Psychological Triggers:**
- Tap into the idea of a SECRET — suggest insider access
- "If you can suggest that there's something secret and you're about to get insider access, people lose their minds over it"
- Use Voice of Customer (VOC) research as the source of all creative language

**Research-First Approach:**
- "If research isn't your biggest task, you're probably doing it wrong"
- Mine Amazon reviews, support tickets, surveys for exact customer language
- The words customers USE are the words that convert

### What Makes Wiebe Unique
Research-driven conversion focus. Every visual and copy decision is grounded in actual customer language (VOC). She bridges the gap between copywriting and visual design by insisting that the WORDS on the image must come from the customer's own vocabulary.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
WIEBE_RULES = {
  rule_of_one: {
    one_reader: true,
    one_big_idea: true,
    one_offer: true,
    one_promise: true
  },
  headline_format: "first_person_with_quotes",  // "I finally got clear skin"
  image_preference: "woman_looking_at_camera",
  psychological_trigger: "secret_insider_access",
  copy_source: "voice_of_customer_research",
  never_do: ["generic_stock_photo", "multiple_offers", "corporate_voice"]
}
```

## 4. STEFAN GEORGI — RMBC Applied to Ad Creative {#4-stefan-georgi}

### Core Mental Model: "RMBC — Research, Mechanism, Brief, Copy"

**Step 1: Research**
- Deep understanding of prospect, their language, pain points, failed solutions
- This is the FOUNDATION — skip this and everything else fails
- Sources: reviews, forums, competitor ads, customer interviews

**Step 2: Mechanism**
- The "real reason" behind why the prospect failed before
- The unique mechanism of HOW your product works differently
- This becomes the HOOK of your ad creative — the surprising "why"
- Example: "The reason diets fail isn't willpower — it's your gut microbiome"

**Step 3: Brief**
- Pre-write the sales argument in bite-sized chunks before designing
- Outline the logical flow: Problem → Failed Solutions → New Mechanism → Proof → Offer
- Each chunk becomes a visual element or text block in the ad

**Step 4: Copy**
- Write using proven templates and modular blocks
- Apply to: Facebook ads, landing pages, emails, VSLs, static ads

### Visual Application of RMBC

For a static ad, RMBC translates to:
1. **R (Research)** → Choose the #1 pain point and exact customer language for headline
2. **M (Mechanism)** → The hook/angle — what makes this product's approach unique
3. **B (Brief)** → The visual layout plan: what goes where, information hierarchy
4. **C (Copy)** → The actual text overlays, headline, CTA

### What Makes Georgi Unique
Systematization of creativity. He turned copywriting from "inspiration art" into a repeatable 4-step manufacturing process. The MECHANISM concept is his most powerful contribution — every winning ad has a unique mechanism that explains WHY this solution works differently.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
GEORGI_RULES = {
  always_start_with: "research",
  must_have_mechanism: true,  // the unique "why it works" angle
  mechanism_placement: "headline_or_subheadline",
  information_flow: ["pain_point", "failed_solutions", "new_mechanism", "proof", "offer"],
  copy_source: "customer_language_from_research",
  brief_before_design: true,  // plan layout before creating
  never_do: ["design_without_research", "generic_benefit_claims", "missing_mechanism"]
}
```

## 5. NICK SHACKELFORD — DTC Ad Creative Strategy {#5-nick-shackelford}

### Core Mental Model: "Creative IS the New Targeting"
With Meta's AI-driven algorithms, audience targeting is nearly dead. The creative itself acts as the targeting mechanism. If your creative speaks to the right person, the algorithm will find them.

### The MGD Framework (Make Good Decisions)
A systematic, algorithm-proof strategy for creating and testing ad creative:

**Key Principles:**
- Show your product and communicate value prop within FIRST 3 SECONDS
- Average dropoff happens around second 5 — you've already lost them
- Use Amazon reviews to kickstart the creative process (real customer language)
- Use tools like Monkey Learn to identify the right words and hooks

**Creative Volume Strategy:**
- Test many variations rapidly
- Static ads are the fastest to produce and test
- Don't over-invest in production — test the CONCEPT first with low-fi creative
- Scale winners, kill losers fast

**Ad Creative Types Shackelford Recommends:**
- Static ads with strong product focus
- Narrative/storytelling ads
- UGC-style content
- Product demonstration
- Before/after transformations

### What Makes Shackelford Unique
$100M+ in Meta ad spend gives him unmatched pattern recognition. His insight that "creative = targeting" fundamentally shifts how you think about ad design. He bridges media buying and creative strategy — the ad creative IS the media strategy.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
SHACKELFORD_RULES = {
  creative_is_targeting: true,
  value_prop_visible_in: "first_3_seconds",
  product_must_be_visible: true,
  copy_source: "amazon_reviews",
  test_volume: "high",  // many variations, fast iterations
  production_quality: "concept_over_polish",
  must_communicate: ["what_product_is", "who_its_for", "why_its_different"],
  never_do: ["hide_product", "slow_reveal", "rely_on_audience_targeting_alone"]
}
```

## 6. BARRY HOTT — Ugly Ads & Thumb-Stop Mastery {#6-barry-hott}

### Core Mental Model: "Ugly Ads Win"
If your marketing is too pretty, it probably won't work on paid social. Polished = instantly recognized as an ad = skipped.

### The Ugly Ads Philosophy

**Why Ugly Works:**
- An ugly ad looks like the content people opened Facebook/Instagram for
- The uglier you make it, the less likely it's immediately perceived as an ad
- Users have trained themselves to skip anything that looks "designed"
- "I don't think you even need a designer for Facebook Ads"

**What "Ugly" Actually Means:**
- Use native creation tools (in-app design, Canva, CapCut)
- Imperfect lighting, natural aesthetics
- Handwritten notes, post-it notes, screenshots
- Meme formats, news-style content
- Text message screenshots
- Simple Canva-style graphics
- "I was a professional photographer... but I choose to make things imperfect"

**The "Sniff Test" — What to Avoid:**
- Heavy branding in the first frame = dead giveaway
- Custom fonts, strong brand colors immediately visible
- Logo prominence that screams "advertisement"
- Over-designed layouts
- Stock photo aesthetics

### 3 Reasons Static Ads Work (Barry's Thesis):
1. Most content on social is now video — static STANDS OUT by being different
2. Thumb-stopping due to ABSENCE of motion is a refreshing pattern interrupt
3. Less competitive inventory = lower CPMs for image ads

### Barry's Static Ad Formats:
- Handwritten note (barely legible = authentic) — proven 3.4X ROAS
- Post-it note style ads
- Screenshot of a text conversation
- Meme formats native to the platform
- Simple product photo with raw, undesigned text overlay
- "Notes app" screenshot style

### What Makes Barry Unique
He flipped the entire creative industry on its head. While everyone chases production value, he proved that AUTHENTICITY beats polish. His $600M+ in managed ad spend validates this counterintuitive approach. The key insight: your ad must FEEL like content, not advertising.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
BARRY_RULES = {
  aesthetic: "deliberately_imperfect",
  design_tools: ["canva", "native_app_tools", "simple_editors"],
  branding: "minimal_to_none",  // no logo in first frame
  formats: [
    "handwritten_note",
    "post_it_note",
    "text_message_screenshot",
    "meme_format",
    "notes_app_screenshot",
    "simple_product_with_raw_text",
    "news_style_content"
  ],
  feel_like: "organic_content_not_ad",
  save_time_on: "design_polish",
  invest_time_on: "hook_and_big_story_idea",
  production_quality: "intentionally_low",
  test: "does_this_look_like_user_content_in_the_feed?",
  never_do: ["heavy_branding", "stock_photos", "over_designed", "custom_fonts_prominent"]
}
```

## 8. ANDREW FARIS — Directness Framework {#8-andrew-faris}

### Core Mental Model: "Directness Wins"
"The more tightly you communicate your product and what it does, the better."

### The Volleyball Analogy
- **Media buying** = the setter (puts the ball in the right place)
- **Creative** = the hitter/spike (the thing that actually scores)
- A great set makes the hitter's job easier, but the CREATIVE is what converts

### The Directness Framework
- Be CLEAR about what the product IS
- Be CLEAR about what it DOES
- Be CLEAR about who it's FOR
- Communicate your value proposition as tightly as possible
- Remove anything that doesn't serve direct communication

### The BPM Method for Facebook Ads
A simple, powerful advertising system for ecommerce that simplifies into 4 parts:
1. Build stable campaigns
2. Keep costs low
3. Drive high-converting traffic
4. Maintain profitable ROAS

### Creative Testing Methodology
- Don't build separate creative testing campaigns
- Launch every new piece of ad creative in the SAME campaigns as evergreen content
- Use bid caps or cost controls to test
- Let the algorithm decide the winner alongside proven creative

### Influences
Faris draws from Ogilvy and Bill Bernbach but applies through the lens of modern DTC brand-building. His approach is a modern distillation of classic advertising wisdom.

### What Makes Faris Unique
Anti-complexity. While others build elaborate creative systems, Faris strips it down to the essentials: say what the product does, show the product, make it clear. His experience as both a brand CEO and agency media buyer gives him a rare dual perspective.

### Actionable Principles for a Design Agent
```
FARIS_RULES = {
  primary_principle: "directness",
  must_answer: [
    "what_is_this_product",
    "what_does_it_do",
    "who_is_it_for",
    "what_should_I_do_next"
  ],
  product_visibility: "prominent",
  message_clarity: "maximum",
  complexity: "minimal",
  testing: "within_existing_campaigns_not_separate",
  never_do: ["clever_over_clear", "hide_the_product", "indirect_messaging", "over_complicate"]
}
```

## 9. STATIC AD CREATIVE TYPE TAXONOMY {#9-static-ad-type-taxonomy}

### The Complete Taxonomy of Static Ad Types

Based on combined research from Dara Denney, Barry Hott, Motion, Common Thread Collective, and industry best practices:

#### Category A: Product-Focused
| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Product Hero** | Clean product photo, neutral/branded background, product is the star | Brand awareness, retargeting |
| **Product Close-up** | Detailed macro shot showing texture, quality, ingredients | Premium/quality positioning |
| **Product in Use** | Product being used by a real person in context | Lifestyle brands, demonstration |
| **Unboxing Style** | Product emerging from packaging, first-impression feel | Gifting, premium positioning |
| **Ingredient/Feature Callout** | Product with annotated arrows/labels pointing to features | Education, differentiation |

#### Category B: Social Proof
| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Testimonial/Quote Card** | Customer quote with photo or avatar | Trust building, middle funnel |
| **Review Screenshot** | Raw screenshot of Amazon/Trustpilot/app store review | Authenticity, bottom funnel |
| **Star Rating Display** | Bold star rating (4.8/5) with review count | Quick trust signal |
| **Press/Media Mention** | Screenshot or styled card of press coverage | Authority, credibility |
| **Social Proof Stack** | Multiple reviews/awards/logos compiled in one image | Overwhelming proof |
| **Before/After** | Side-by-side transformation with real results | Results-driven products |

#### Category C: Comparison & Education
| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Us vs. Them** | Side-by-side comparison chart against competitors | Differentiation |
| **Listicle/Benefits List** | 3-5 bullet benefits with icons or checkmarks | Feature-rich products |
| **Problem-Solution** | Visual showing problem on top, product as solution below | Pain point awareness |
| **Myth-Busting** | "You've been told X... but actually Y" format | Paradigm-shifting products |
| **How It Works** | 3-step numbered process showing simplicity | Complex products made simple |
| **Infographic Style** | Data visualization of key claims | Data-driven audiences |

#### Category D: Emotional & Story
| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Founder's Story** | Photo of founder with origin story snippet | Brand building, connection |
| **Lifestyle Aspiration** | Person living the desired outcome (no product focus) | Top of funnel, aspiration |
| **Problem Agitation** | Visual that dramatizes the pain point | Cold audience awareness |
| **Curiosity Hook** | Intriguing image with teaser text that demands the click | Traffic campaigns |

#### Category E: Native/Ugly (Barry Hott Style)
| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Handwritten Note** | Handwritten message on paper, photographed | Authenticity, pattern interrupt |
| **Post-it Note** | Simple message on a post-it | Quick message, thumb-stop |

| **Meme Format** | Current meme template adapted for product | Viral potential, engagement |
| **Notes App Screenshot** | iPhone notes app with product info | Native, unpolished feel |

#### Category F: Offer & Urgency
| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Sale/Discount** | Bold typography showing offer (40% OFF) | Promotions, BFCM |
| **Bundle/Value Stack** | Multiple products shown together with total value | AOV increase |
| **Limited Edition** | Scarcity-focused creative with urgency elements | Seasonal, launches |
| **Free Shipping/Bonus** | Highlight of additional value included | Conversion boost |

## 10. NEUROMARKETING PRINCIPLES FOR AD DESIGN {#10-neuromarketing}

### The 6 Stimuli That Speak to the "Old Brain" (Decision-Making Center)

Based on CXL research and neuromarketing studies:

1. **Self-Centered** — The old brain responds to messages about ITSELF. Use "you" language. Show the viewer in the ad (or someone they identify with).

2. **Contrast** — The brain is wired to notice DIFFERENCES, not absolutes. Before/after, with/without, us/them — contrast accelerates decision-making.

3. **Tangible** — Abstract concepts don't register. Show concrete, tangible outcomes. Numbers, images of real results, physical products.

4. **Beginning & End** — The brain pays most attention at the START and END of any experience. Your opening image and CTA are the two most critical elements.

5. **Visual** — Images are processed 60,000x faster than text. The brain picks up images and high-impact visuals more than text. Lead with visual, support with words.

6. **Emotional** — Emotional responses are processed faster than rational ones. Create a feeling FIRST, then support with logic.

### The 1.5-Second Rule
- You have 1.5 seconds to stop the scroll
- Recent studies show digital ads get less than 2.5 seconds of total attention
- The brain decides "relevant or not" in under 1 second
- EVERYTHING important must be communicated in the first visual impression

### Face Detection Priority
- The human brain is WIRED to detect faces — it's hardwired from birth
- Faces with direct eye contact trigger social attention automatically
- Emotional expressions create empathy and connection
- A well-done ad will have a person clearly expressing an emotion

### The Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)
- Items that stand out from their surroundings are remembered better
- In a feed of polished content, an "ugly" ad stands out
- In a feed of video, a static image stands out
- Novelty captures attention — be DIFFERENT from surrounding content

### Specific Neuromarketing Data Points
- Warm colors (red, orange, yellow) get 4.8% CTR vs cool colors at lower rates
- High-contrast ads get 5.4% CTR and 3.6% conversion rate
- Eye tracking shows people look at faces before anything else in an ad
- Red triggers arousal/attraction; blue triggers calm/cognitive control

## 11. VISUAL HIERARCHY & EYE-TRACKING PATTERNS {#11-visual-hierarchy}

### The Three Questions Every Ad Must Answer Instantly
1. **What is this?** (product/service identification)
2. **Who is it for?** (relevance to viewer)
3. **What should I do next?** (clear CTA)

### Z-Pattern Layout (Best for Ads with Visual + Text)
```
[1. HOOK/IMAGE] -----> [2. LOGO/BRAND]
        \
         \  (diagonal sweep)
          \
[3. SUPPORTING INFO] --> [4. CTA BUTTON]
```
- Entry: top-left (primary optical area) — place your HOOK here
- Sweep: top-right (strong fallow area) — brand mark
- Diagonal: natural eye movement to bottom-left
- Exit: bottom-right (terminal area) — place CTA here

### F-Pattern (For Text-Heavy Static Ads)
```
[========== TOP LINE READ FULLY ===========]
[======= SECOND LINE SHORTER ======]
[|]
[|] (scanning down left side)
[|]
```
- Top line gets full reading — put your HEADLINE here
- Second line gets partial reading — SUBHEADLINE
- Left column gets vertical scanning — BENEFIT BULLETS

### The 3-Point Focal System
High-converting ads have exactly 3 primary focal points:
1. **Primary**: Headline or hero image (gets 50% of attention)
2. **Secondary**: Product or proof element (gets 30% of attention)
3. **Tertiary**: CTA or offer (gets 20% of attention)

More than 3 focal points = confusion. Fewer than 2 = boring.

### Visual Weight Rules
| Element | Visual Weight |
|---------|--------------|
| High contrast against background | 95% |
| Isolated element with whitespace | 100% |
| Crowded element with clutter | 30% |
| Large element | Higher weight |
| Bright/saturated color | Higher weight |
| Human face | Highest natural weight |

### Mobile-First Design Principles (98% of Meta users are mobile)
- Large text readable on small screens (minimum 24pt equivalent)
- Vertical (4:5) or square (1:1) formats fill mobile screens
- Simple designs — detail gets lost on 6" screens
- Tap-friendly CTA zones
- No tiny text or fine details

## 12. COLOR THEORY FOR ADS {#12-color-theory}

### The Contrast Principle (Most Important for Ads)
- The human eye processes CONTRAST before any other visual detail
- Black text on white = 70% higher readability than low-contrast
- High-contrast ads get 5.4% CTR vs lower for low-contrast
- Your CTA must be the HIGHEST CONTRAST element in the ad

### Complementary Color Strategy for CTAs
Use the color wheel — opposite colors create maximum contrast:
| Dominant Color | CTA/Accent Color |
|---------------|------------------|
| Blue | Orange |
| Purple | Yellow |
| Green | Red |
| Dark backgrounds | Bright white/yellow CTA |

> "In a sea of orange, your eye notices blue faster than any other color."

### Color Emotion Mapping for Ad Context
| Color | Triggers | Best For |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Red | Urgency, appetite, passion | Sales, food, urgency CTAs |
| Orange | Confidence, warmth, action | CTAs, energy products |
| Yellow | Optimism, attention, warning | Highlights, callouts |
| Green | Health, nature, money, growth | Health products, finance |
| Blue | Trust, calm, professional | Tech, corporate, trust signals |
| Purple | Luxury, creativity, wisdom | Premium products |
| Black | Sophistication, luxury, power | Premium, fashion, minimalism |
| White | Clean, simple, pure | Backgrounds, medical, clarity |

### The Accent Color Rule
- Your accent color should be the MOST SATURATED, highest-contrast color in the palette
- Use it ONLY for the most important element (CTA, headline, key number)
- Everything else should be lower saturation to create hierarchy
- Orange and green CTA buttons generate 32% higher click rates than neutral tones

### Feed Context Rule
- Your ad competes with the feed's default white/light gray background
- To stand out: use bold, saturated backgrounds that BREAK the feed pattern
- OR: use bold text/elements on a white background with extreme contrast
- Test dark backgrounds (unusual in feed) for pattern interruption

## 13. GESTALT PRINCIPLES APPLIED TO ADS {#13-gestalt}

### Proximity
Elements that are CLOSE TOGETHER are perceived as belonging together.
- **Ad Application**: Group related info (price + product = they go together). Separate headline from body with clear space. Keep CTA isolated with whitespace to make it prominent.

### Similarity
Elements that LOOK ALIKE are perceived as part of the same group.
- **Ad Application**: Use consistent colors/shapes for benefit bullets. Make your CTA button a DIFFERENT shape/color so it breaks similarity and stands out.

### Closure
The brain fills in missing visual information to complete a pattern.
- **Ad Application**: You don't need to show the ENTIRE product. Cropped images create curiosity. Partial reveals invite the brain to complete the picture (engagement).

### Figure/Ground
We naturally separate foreground (figure) from background (ground).
- **Ad Application**: Your product/message MUST be the figure. Background should recede. Use blur, desaturation, or simplicity in background. Never let background compete with foreground.

### Continuity
The eye follows the smoothest path through visual elements.
- **Ad Application**: Arrange elements in a flow that guides the eye from headline → image → benefit → CTA. Use lines, arrows, or visual direction to create flow.

### Pragnanz (Simplicity)
The brain prefers the simplest possible interpretation.
- **Ad Application**: If your ad requires effort to understand, you've already lost. One concept. One visual. One message. Make it INSTANTLY parseable.

## 14. COGNITIVE BIASES FOR AD DESIGN {#14-cognitive-biases}

### Biases That Work in Static Ad Creative

**1. Anchoring Bias**
- Show original price crossed out next to sale price
- "Was $97 → Now $47" anchors perception of value
- Tiered pricing in the ad (show premium option to make standard look reasonable)
- Place the BIGGER number first, always

**2. Social Proof (Bandwagon Effect)**
- Star ratings displayed prominently (4.8/5 from 12,000 reviews)
- "Best-seller" or "#1 in category" badges
- User count ("Join 50,000+ happy customers")
- Logos of press mentions or "As Seen In"

**3. Scarcity & Urgency**
- "Only 3 left in stock" — limited supply signals
- Countdown timer elements in static images
- "Limited edition" labels
- "Ends tonight" overlays
- Scarcity of TIME or QUANTITY both trigger action

**4. Loss Aversion**
- People feel losses 2x more intensely than equivalent gains
- Frame as "Don't miss out" rather than "Get this deal"
- Show what they're LOSING by not acting (not just gaining)
- "You're spending $X/month on products that don't work"

**5. Authority Bias**
- Doctor/expert endorsements visible in creative
- Clinical study results ("Clinically proven...")
- Certification badges, awards, patents
- Expert photo with credentials

**6. The Framing Effect**
- "95% fat-free" performs better than "5% fat" — same fact, different frame
- Presentation matters more than content
- "Limited edition" with bold, urgent colors triggers scarcity
- Positive framing for benefits, negative framing for problems

**7. The Curiosity Gap**
- Show just ENOUGH to intrigue, not enough to satisfy
- Teaser text: "The #1 mistake people make with..."
- Partial product reveals
- Blurred or cropped images that invite "what is that?"

**8. The Halo Effect**
- Beautiful product photography elevates perceived quality
- Association with respected brands/people transfers credibility
- High-quality design (when appropriate) signals premium
- Packaging design visible = brand equity transfer

## 15. THE UNIFIED STATIC AD DESIGN FRAMEWORK {#15-unified-framework}

### Combining ALL Mental Models Into One Actionable System

#### PHASE 1: PRE-DESIGN (Research — Georgi + Wiebe + Shackelford)
```
Before designing ANYTHING:
1. Define the ONE reader (Wiebe Rule of One)
2. Extract customer language from reviews/research (Georgi Research + Wiebe VOC)
3. Identify the #1 pain point and the MECHANISM (Georgi Mechanism)
4. Define the ONE offer and ONE promise (Wiebe Rule of One)
5. Choose the ad type from the taxonomy (Denney 10 Types)
```

#### PHASE 2: LAYOUT (Ogilvy + Eye-Tracking + Gestalt)
```
Layout Rules:
1. Image/visual dominates 60-75% of ad (Ogilvy)
2. Follow Z-pattern or F-pattern based on content density
3. Maximum 3 focal points (visual hierarchy)
4. Headline below or ON the main image (Ogilvy)
5. CTA in the terminal area (bottom-right for Z-pattern)
6. Use proximity to group related elements (Gestalt)
7. Isolate CTA with whitespace for maximum weight (Gestalt)
8. 4:5 vertical or 1:1 square format for mobile (mobile-first)
```

#### PHASE 3: VISUAL DESIGN (Barry + Neuromarketing + Color Theory)
```
Design Decisions:
1. Decide aesthetic: POLISHED or UGLY based on funnel stage
   - TOF cold audience → Ugly/native (Barry)
   - BOF retargeting → Polished/product-focused (Ogilvy)
2. Use high-contrast colors (neuromarketing data: 5.4% CTR)
3. CTA button uses COMPLEMENTARY color to dominant palette
4. Faces with eye contact when possible (neuro: face detection priority)
5. Product MUST be visible (Shackelford + Faris)
6. Background recedes, product/message is figure (Gestalt figure/ground)
```

#### PHASE 4: COPY ON IMAGE (Sullivan + Wiebe + Faris)
```
Text Rules:
1. Headline: 2-5 words maximum (Faris directness)
2. Use customer's own words (Wiebe VOC)
3. First-person with quotation marks for testimonials (Wiebe)
4. Include a MECHANISM or unique angle (Georgi)
5. If possible, the image tells the story WITHOUT text (Sullivan)
6. Never all-caps headlines (Ogilvy)
7. Maximum 2 typefaces (Ogilvy)
8. Sentence case, not title case (Ogilvy)
```

#### PHASE 5: THUMB-STOP CHECK (Barry + Neuromarketing)
```
Before publishing, pass these tests:
□ Does this look like USER CONTENT, not an ad? (Barry sniff test)
□ Can you understand the message in 1.5 seconds? (neuro 1.5s rule)
□ Does it answer: What is it? Who is it for? What do I do? (hierarchy test)
□ Is there ONE clear focal point? (Gestalt pragnanz)
□ Does it CONTRAST with typical feed content? (Von Restorff effect)
□ Is the product visible? (Shackelford + Faris)
□ Is there a clear CTA? (conversion principle)
□ Would you stop scrolling for this? (honest gut check)
```

#### PHASE 6: VARIATION STRATEGY (Denney + Shackelford)
```
For every concept, produce:
1. Minimum 3 hook variations (Denney 3-Part Hook)
2. Test across multiple ad types from taxonomy
3. Test ugly vs. polished versions
4. Rotate winning creative every 21-28 days (Denney)
5. Launch in existing campaigns, not separate test campaigns (Faris)
6. Track winning "creative recipes" (Denney)
```

## QUICK REFERENCE: THE 20 COMMANDMENTS OF STATIC AD DESIGN

1. **ONE message per ad** — if it needs a paragraph, it's too complex
2. **Product must be visible** — never hide what you're selling
3. **1.5 seconds to communicate** — if it takes longer, you've lost
4. **Ugly beats pretty** for cold audiences on social
5. **Faces with eye contact** stop thumbs faster than anything else
6. **Headline below the image**, never above (10% more readers)
7. **Maximum 3 focal points** — headline, product, CTA
8. **CTA uses complementary color** to dominant palette for maximum contrast
9. **Customer language, not brand language** — mine reviews for exact words
10. **Every winning ad has a MECHANISM** — the unique "why it works"
11. **Vertical 4:5 or square 1:1** for mobile-first design
12. **Never all-caps headlines** — sentence case only
13. **Maximum 2 typefaces** — one for headlines, one for body
14. **Show RESULTS, not features** — finished dish, not raw ingredients
15. **Social proof is visual currency** — star ratings, review counts, press logos
16. **Scarcity triggers action** — limited time/quantity signals work
17. **Test 10 creative types** before declaring an angle dead
18. **3 hook variations** minimum per concept
19. **Rotate every 21-28 days** to avoid fatigue
20. **Directness wins** — say what it is, what it does, who it's for
